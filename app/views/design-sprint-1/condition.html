Chlamydia is the most common sexually transmitted infection (STI). Men and women can get it.

You can have chlamydia even if you don't have any symptoms, or your symptoms go away.

It's easy to treat.

If you don't treat chlamydia, it can spread and cause complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease, lower fertility or infertility.

The only way to know if you have chlamydia is to get a test. Anyone can get a test for free.

Symptoms of chlamydia

Women

The symptoms of chlamydia in women are:

  • bleeding between periods (even if you use hormonal contraception like the pill or an implant)
  • bleeding after or during sex
  • pain during sex
  • pelvic pain
  • pain when you pee
  • discharge from your bottom
  • pain, swelling irritation and discharge in your eyes (conjunctivitis)

Men

The symptoms of chlamydia in men are:

  • discharge from your penis or bottom
  • pain when you pee
  • pain in your testicles
  • pain, swelling irritation and discharge in your eyes (conjunctivitis)

People at risk

Anyone who has sex can get chlamydia and pass it on. You don't need to have lots of sex with lots of people to be at risk.

You're more at risk and should get a test if any of the following apply:

  • you have (or think you might have) symptoms
  • someone you've had sex with has (or thinks they might have) symptoms
  • you've recently had unprotected sex with a new partner
  • you, or your sexual partner, have had unprotected sex with other partners
  • someone you've had sex with tells you they have chlamydia, or a sexually transmitted infection
  • you have another sexually transmitted infection
  • you're pregnant or planning a pregnancy

If someone you've had sex with has a test for chlamydia and the results are negative, you could still have chlamydia. This is because tests:

  • can be wrong - they’re right over 90% of the time
  • may not detect the infection if the test is done too soon after unprotected sex

How long you should wait before you get a test

You should go straight away if you think you might have chlamydia. You shouldn’t wait for symptoms, because you might not get any.

The infection could take up to 2 weeks to appear, so you may be told to have another test 2 weeks after you had unprotected sex.

How chlamydia is treated

If you have chlamydia, you will be given one of the following:

  • azithromycin – 2 or 4 tablets at once
  • doxycycline – 2 capsules a day for a week

If it's likely that you have the infection, a nurse or doctor may give you the treatment before you get your test results. This might happen if you have symptoms, or someone you’ve had sex with has chlamydia.

What causes chlamydia

Chlamydia is a bacteria that's found in people who have the infection. It can live in your:

  • cervix (entrance to the womb)
  • urethra (tube where your pee comes out)
  • bottom
  • throat
  • eyes - contact with infected semen or vaginal fluid can cause conjunctivitis

The infection is most commonly spread by:

  • unprotected vaginal, anal or oral sex
  • sharing sex toys - if you don't wash them or cover them with a new condom

Chlamydia can also be passed from a pregnant woman to her baby.

You can't get chlamydia from kissing, hugging, swimming pools, toilet seats, sharing baths, towels, cups, plates or cutlery.
