1. About chlamydia

Chlamydia is the most common sexually transmitted infection (STI). Men and women can get it.

Anyone who has unprotected sex can get chlamydia.

You could have chlamydia even if you don't have any symptoms, or your symptoms go away.

The only way to know if you have it is to get a chlamydia test.

It's easy to treat with medical help.

2. Symptoms

Women

The symptoms of chlamydia in women are:

  • bleeding between periods (even if you use contraception)
  • vaginal discharge that's not usual for you
  • bleeding after or during sex
  • pain when you pee or have sex
  • pelvic pain
  • discharge from your bottom
  • pain, swelling and discharge in your eyes (conjunctivitis)

Men

The symptoms of chlamydia in men are:

  • discharge from your penis or bottom
  • pain when you pee
  • pain in your testicles
  • pain, swelling and discharge in your eyes (conjunctivitis)

If you think you have any symptoms you should get a test.

3. People at risk

Anyone who has sex can get chlamydia and pass it on. You don't need to have sex with lots of people to be at risk.

You're more at risk and should get a chlamydia test if any of the following apply:

  • you've had unprotected sex with a new partner
  • you or your sexual partner had unprotected sex with someone else
  • someone you've had sex with thinks they have symptoms
  • someone you've had sex with has recently had a test for chlamydia
  • someone you've had sex with tells you they have chlamydia or a sexually transmitted infection (STI)
  • you have a different STI
  • you're pregnant or planning a pregnancy
If you think you're at risk, get a chlamydia test.

4. Causes of chlamydia

Chlamydia is a bacteria that's found in people who have the infection. It can live in the:

  • cervix (entrance to the womb)
  • urethra (tube that your pee comes out)
  • bottom
  • throat
  • eyes - contact with infected semen or vaginal fluid can cause conjunctivitis

Chlamydia is usually passed on by:

  • having unprotected vaginal, anal or oral sex
  • sharing sex toys - if you don't wash them or cover them with a new condom

Chlamydia can also be passed from a pregnant woman to her baby.

When you won't catch chlamydia

You can't get chlamydia from kissing, hugging, swimming pools, toilet seats, sharing baths, towels, cups, plates or cutlery.

5. Treatment

If you have chlamydia a health professional will give you antibiotics. It's usually one of the following:

  • azithromycin - you'll take 2 or 4 tablets at once, by mouth
  • doxycycline - you'll take 2 capsules a day for 1 week, by mouth

You might be given antibiotics before you get your test results. This could happen if it's likely that you have chlamydia, for example you have symptoms.

After you've finished the antibiotics, you may be told to have another chlamydia test to check it's gone.
